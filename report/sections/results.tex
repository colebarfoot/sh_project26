\section{Results and Discussion}
The parallel arrays had an average performance of 0.982 ns day$^{-1}$
for 4000000 timesteps, achieving a total simulation time of 4 ns.

\par
Ehrenfest's first-order phase transition is a discontinuity in the first derivative of the
free energy with respect to thermodynamic quantities \cite{jaeger1998}.
Hence, identification of phase transitions can be made by plotting the free energy of a system
against whichever intensive quantity (pressure, temperature, or volume) is being varied.
Free energy calculations, however, are rarely straightforward since determining the entropy 
term is challenging (or practically impossible).
There are other quantities which may be measured, although the variation of which may be more subtle.

\par
Since Ice--VII and --VIII are known to have different unit cell dimensions, there are geometric
properties that can be used to differentiate them.
Structural properties may also be monitored with the radial distribution function (RDF) $g(r)$.
The RDF describes the probability that an atom may be found at a distance $r$ from a central atom.
It is given by the density distribution $\rho(r)$ normalised against some `bulk mean density' $\rho$ such that
$g(r) = \rho(r)/\rho$ \cite{goharshadi2025}.
RDFs for water molecules are typically partitioned into three parts: $g_\mathrm{OO}(R)$, $g_\mathrm{OH}(r)$, and
$g_\mathrm{HH}(r)$, where the first subscript represents the central type atom and the second subscript represents
the neighbour atom type.
Analysis of these functions should reveal key features such as the the OH distribution peaks at the length of the
covalent bond and the hydrogen bond ($\approx$1\AA{}), and the HH distribution peak at the distance between two hydrogen atoms in
the same molecule ($\approx$1.5\AA{}).
Moreover, different levels of hydrogen-ordering should exhibit different total energy configurations.

\par
Plastic Ice--VII should not exhibit significant geometric differences to Ice--VII, so identifying this phase with
any degree of accuracy requires a certain amount of nuance.
It is predicted that the water molecules in this phase are free to spin themselves about, with the hydrogen
atoms displacing themselves from their original position in which case there would be an expected increase
of the hydrogen mean squared displacement $\langle r^2 \rangle _\mathrm{H}$.

\subsection{VII/VIII Phase Boundary}
As previously discussed, from the phase diagram in Figure \ref{fig:phase-diagram}, the phase boundary for these
3 GPa simulations lies around 273K.
This transition is known to display hysteresis (some stubbornness to transition) \cite{pruzan1994} due to the 
challenge of reorienting protons from different orderings while simultaneously obeying the Bernal-Fowler rules.
Consider one water molecule rotating such that its protons become ordered with respect to one of its neighbours. 
Its new position will be breaking the rules: either there will be two hydrogens opposed to one another, or 
there will be more than two hydrogens at an oxygen site.
This will either force the molecule to rotate back to its initial position, or send the next
molecule into a similar rotation.
As conditions become more extreme, these sort of random motions begin to happen with more frequency
and eventually the phase transition will be much more likely to take place \cite{ackland2025}.

\par
In Figure \ref{fig:enthalpy}, at around 430K both a sudden change of the unit cell geometry of the Ice--VIII
system may be observed. 
Below 430K, the lattice parameter ratio is approximately $\frac{c}{a} = 1.45$ (multiplying by $\sqrt{2}$).
Comparing with the experimental value obtained by neutron diffraction by Kuhs \textit{et al.}, $\frac{c}{a}=1.4551(5)$, 
AMOEBA ice is in good agreement.
At 430K, the distorted orthorhombic structure may be allowed to return to the BCC structure of Ice--VII 
structure as the protons ondergo a disordering transition and the lattice parameters become approximately
equal as expected. 
Above the transition temperature, Ice--VII maintains its BCC structure with the lattice parameter ratio remaining
approximately constant until around 520K.
As the temperature increases, the enthalpy per atom increases linearly as expected.
Below the transition temperature, the Ice--VIII structure has a lower enthalpy per atom which can be attributed
to the more stable proton-ordered configuration. 
This enthalpy well also will contribute to the hysteresis of the transition by increasing the height of the
energy barrier.
At the transition temperature, the enthalpies of the two systems can be seen to coincide and are approximately 
equal above this point, as would be anticipated if the two phases were identical.
At around 520K, there is a discontinuity in the enthalpy, which can be identified as the melting point of Ice--VII.
With reference to the phase diagram (Figure \ref{fig:phase-diagram}), this value is about 100K greater than that
predicted by calculation.
This suggests that the intermolecular forces in the AMOEBA model are significantly stronger at high pressure than
the real value.
Importantly, however, the model has allowed the crystal to undergo the predicted phase transitions.
The adjusting of parameters to fine-tune for high pressure could shift the observed transition temperatures
closer to the experimental and calculated values.

\par
The RDFs in Figure \ref{fig:rdfoverlay} contains several key features by which the structure of AMOEBA ice can 
be assessed. 
For all curves, there is a widening of the peaks with increasing thermal excitation, as expected.
Each curve provides useful information about the structure, representing different kinds of bonding:

\par
The sharp peak in the OH RDF at about 1\AA{}  represents the intramolecular O---H covalent bonding which is the
expected bond length, with the literature value $b_0 = ?$.
The second peak can be identified as the two hydrogens which are hydrogen-bonded to the central oxygen in the
tetrahedral structure predicted by the Bernal-Fowler rules at a bond distance of about 1.9\AA{}.
As the separation from the oxygen atom increases, peaks corresponding to the hydrogen atoms covalently bonded
to the neighbouring oxygens in the tetrahedral network and further neighbours are observed.
Notably, the curves for both Ice--VII and Ice--VIII have similar shapes, which suggests that both structures
exhibit the tetrahedral structure required by the ice rules with hydrogen- and covalent-bonding lengths remaining
approximately the same.
However, due to the tetragonal distortion of the Ice--VIII structure, the low-temperature peaks become wider
with neighbouring oxygen atoms further apart.
Another interesting feature is the smoothing of the 3\AA{} peak with an increase in thermal excitation.
As the hydrogen atoms are vibrating with greater amplitude, the peak becomes smeared.
Note, this peak smoothing is not indicative of a phase transition.

\par
The first peak in the HH RDF can be identified as the distance between the two hydrogen atoms inside the water
molecule at about 1.5\AA{}.
Using the $b_0$ from before and the expected bond angle of $104.5^\circ$, this value is sensible.
Some widening of this peak can be observed, which can be attributed to the AMOEBA model allowing the molecule
to slightly bend and stretch.
This variation is not as significant as observed in experiment.
The next peak represents the hydrogen atoms covalently bonded to the oxygen atom hydrogen-bonded to the central
hydrogen atom
With increasing temperature, the peak widens corresponding to stretching of the hydrogen-bond but this effect
is far greater than for the covalent bond. 
This is consistent with the `flexibility' of the hydrogen-bond.
Beyond this second peak, the Ice--VIII structure has more defined wells and peaks at low temperatures than
Ice--VII which corresponds to the long-range proton ordering.

\par
In the OO RDF, the first peak of the two structures, which represents the oxygen atoms on neighbouring 
lattice points, has a key difference: Ice--VII exhibits a minor peak at about 3.2\AA{} which is characteristic
of a BCC structure but this disappears with the distortion into the tetragonal structure of Ice--VIII.
The BCC minor peak appears because the lattice point on the base of the unit cell is a distance $(\sqrt{2}/2)a$ \AA{}
from the origin and the lattice point on the cube vertex is a distance $a$ \AA{} from the origin, but upon distortion
the difference between the positions of these lattice points shrinks.
The Ice--VIII structure also exhibits a double peak between 5 and 6\AA{}, which is around the length of the $c$ parameter,
corresponding to the $\epsilon/2$ shifting.

\par
At the temperatures above the phase transition, 450K and 500K, $g(r)$ for both systems become the same as the Ice--VIII
protons become disordered and the structure is allowed to relax.

\par 
The mean squared displacements (MSD) of the hydrogen and oxygen atoms of both systems in Figure \ref{fig:msd}
have some interesting features.

\par
Below phase transition temperature, the hydrogen MSD for Ice--VIII is at an almost constant value
of about 0.3\AA{} which would be expected for vibrational motion in a fixed position.
At the phase transition, the MSD changes suddenly to around 1.8\AA{} which corresponds to the
distance of water molecule rotation about the lattice point, while maintaining the tetrahedral
bonding.
This is consisted with the predicted order $\to$ disorder transition from VIII $\to$ VII.
Above the transition, the hydrogen MSD again remains approximately constant with a stable post-transition
structure.
As expected with no identified phase-transition, the hydrogen MSD of Ice--VII is initially approximately 
the same for all temperatures at about 0.2\AA{}. 
At a temperature slightly lower than the VIII $\to$ VII transition, however, there is a similar sudden 
change.
Although this new structure must still be Ice--VII, below this temperature the ice must have been in a metastable
amorphous-type structure.
It appears that with enough thermal energy, the water molecules are able to reorient into another, more energetically 
favourable disorded state.
Above this `deglassification' temperature, the new structure is seemingly stable with no further MSD variation.

\par
The oxygen MSD for Ice--VIII follows a similar trend to the hydrogen MSD. 
Below the transition temperature, the MSD is approximately a constant value and then as the ice undergoes
the phase transition the oxygen atoms settle into the positions of the undistorted lattice.
As the hydrogen atoms begin to rotate about the oxygen atoms, the oxygen atoms have less space to move
and vibrate which corresponds to a decrease in MSD.
The Ice--VII structure exhibits this same phenonmenon with a decrease in MSD until a minimum at
around 450K.
From this point, the MSD begins to increase with temperature as thermal excitation of the molecules increases
the amplitude of their vibrations.

\par
Since the VIII $\to$ VII phase transition can be described as a rotation of the hydrogen atoms about the 
oxygen atoms, plotting the hydrogen MSD as a function of time describes how this rotation process occurs
in practice.
Figure \ref{fig:hmsdvarpre} is this plot for the Ice--VIII system at 430K, just below the transition temperature
identified earlier, but still far greater than the real-world value.
As the simulation progresses, the MSD displacement is trending upwards approximately linearly.
There are small peaks and dips which represent pockets of molecules which begin to reorient and subsequently
return to their original positions because of the restrictions of the Bernal-Fowler rules.
Although this Ice--VIII does not undergo a full phase transition, the trend is towards slow, complete reorientation,
\textit{i.e.} with more simulation time, it is likely that this system will transition.
Figure \ref{fig:hmsdvarpost}, however, is the plot for the same system at just above the transition temperature
at 440K.
This system exhibits much less back-and-forth reorientation, and does so much more quickly than the lower temperature
system.
Clearly, this process is still a gradual, linear reorientation but begins to taper and reach the stable value of
$\approx$1.7\AA{} towards the end of the simulation.
Hence, it may be suggested that the observed hysteresis would reduce given signicantly more simulation time.
The computational limits of the AMOEBA model then are a practical issue for MD simulations to agree with calculated
and experimental values.

\subsection{Plastic Ice-VII}
At a certain temperature, we expect the Ice--VII structure to allow its molecules to `freely' rotate their hydrogen
atoms while the oxygen atoms remain in their fixed lattice parameters, the `plastic' Ice--VII phase.
This rotation is not entirely free, because the molecules are still densely packed and effects such as 
electrostatic repulsion between opposed protons and hydrogen bonding still take place.
The transition plastic phase also is not accompanied by a discontinuity in the first derivative of the free energy,
nor any shift in the unit cell geometry, so its identification is challenging.
Simulations were done in the same format as the VII/VIII phase boundary runs, but at 8 GPa and at higher 
temperatures in order to investigate this phenomenon.

In Figure \ref{fig:enthalpy7p}, as predicted, the enthalpy changes linearly as would be expected with increasing
temperature with no discontinuities.
At about 670K, however, the enthalpy appears to have a sudden jump similar to the lower pressure Ice--VII which
corresponds to a melting transition.
This result is consistent with the a higher calculated melting point for higher pressure.
The cell geometry also remains cubic, with a $c/a$ ratio of $\approx$1. 
At the melting point, the box dimensions begin to fluctuate with as the molecules begin to move about freely.

\par
The RDFs for the high-pressure, high-temperature run of Ice--VII in Figure \ref{fig:rdfoverlay7p}
are very similar to the previous run.
The peaks representing the key features of covalent- and hydrogen-bonding and the BCC unit cell
structure are observed.
Beacuse this run is started at high temperature, there are none of the low-temperature minor peaks.
In all of the simulations there is enough thermal excitation that the curves have been smoothed.
At the highest temperature, in the melting transition, all of the small peaks begin to flatten out
towards a constant value of 1.
Recalling the definition of the radial distribution function, this corresponds to the density distribution
$\rho(r)$ tending to the `mean-field' density $\rho$, \textit{i.e.} all molecules are randomly distributed
about the central atom as in a liquid.

\par
Since neither the RDF nor the enthalpy provide sufficient detail to identify any sort of 
change, the most sensible quantity to examine is the MSD in Figure \ref{fig:msd7p}.
At low temperature, the hydrogen MSD is approximately constant at $\approx$1\AA{} before quickly
inreasing with temperature up to some constant value between 460K and 510K.
This `plateau' is significantly long, stretching between around 510K and 650K, with a constant value
of $\approx$2.3\AA{}. 
This value is greater than the constant high-temperature MSD value achieved in the lower pressure
run.
This could be attributed to hydrogen rotations about the oxygen atoms on the lattice points of a
greater magnitude than a reorientation from some metastable Ice--VII state as before.
These larger rotations may be characteristic of a plastic phase, although in order to confirm this,
more evidence is required.
Above this plateau, the hydrogen MSD begins to increase rapidly with the identified melting transition.
The hydrogen MSD increase throughout the run is accompanied by a reduction in the oxygen MSD
as observed in the low-pressure run.
This is most likely due to the greater motion of the hydrogen atoms, allowing less space to the oxygen
atoms for movement.
As the melting point is reached, the oxygen atoms begin to disperse rapidly from their initial
positions as expected.
