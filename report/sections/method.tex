\section{Method}

There are many different common molecular dynamics engines such as GROMACS but the vehicule of choice 
for the ice simulations in this report is LAMMPS (Large-scale Atomic/Molecular Massively Parallel Simulator).
This choice was motivated by two factors: it is highly computationally efficient, with native support for 
OpenMPI and OpenMP, and it recently implemented the AMOEBA force field, 
originally only available on the TINKER software.
LAMMPS has extensive documentation and a set of tutorials and exercises that allowed rapid familiarisation
with the software.
Once the AMOEBA simulations were complete, all of the data was analysed with the author's original Python code, all of
which has been made publically available \cite{github}.

\par
The AMOEBA water was parameterised using the data from Ren and Ponder's original model \cite{ren2003}.
The ice structures were generated using GenIce2 and then loaded into LAMMPS: these crystals are shown
in Figure \ref{fig:init} \cite{matsumoto2024,gravelle2025}.
For Ice--VII there were 2000 molecules and for Ice--VIII there were 1728.
This number is sufficient for simulating long-range coordination effects in the condensed phase, with
at least 5 molecules extending in all spatial directions from the central atom at which point interactions
begin to drop off.

\par
It is important to note, however, that this number of molecules is still relatively tiny.
For reference, the number of water molecules in an average glass of water is around $10^{25}$!
Hence, a real periodic crystal extends in all three spatial dimensions for many orders of magnitude
worth of atoms.
In order to eliminate grain boundary and crystal defect effects, such as a lowered melting point, 
periodic boundary conditions must be imposed.
This ensures that the simulation treats the crystal system as fictitiously extending periodically
beyond the simulation box boundaries \cite{sharma2019}.

\par
Implementing MD in practice is a balancing act;
maintaining the stability of a system with so many different moving parts can be difficult.
For example, if one molecule were placed on top of another, the Pauli and electrostatic repulsions would send the two
flying off into space immediately.
Additionally, if the simulation box were defined to be too small for the number of atoms, the simulation pressure and energy
would immediately become massive and unphysical behaviour might occur.
Hence, precise setup of boundary and initial conditions is important.

\par
Care also needs to be taken with maintaining the conserved quantities of the simulation: \textit{e.g.}
temperature, pressure and volume.
Adjustments that are too rapid and/or aggresive may destabilize the system.
Hence, two of the key tools for MD simulations are thermostats and barostats: functions which maintain
the temperature and pressure, respectively, in a controlled way.
These simulations are carried out in the NPT ensemble (number of particles, pressure, and temperature conserved)
using Nosé-Hoover chain equations of motion outlined by Shinoda \textit{et al.} \cite{shinoda2004}.
The thermostat rescales the particle velocities to maintain the temperature and the barostat rescales the 
simulation box dimensions to maintain the components of the system's internal stress tensor. 
Both act on a timescale defined by some damping parameter.
For both the isotropic and anisotropic variants of the barostat, this stress tensor is diagonal, however the isotropic
tensor has identical components down the diagonal.
The anisotropic barostat is allowed to adjust the three dimensions of the simulation box disproportionately
to one another.
Because these controls are damped, it takes some time for oscillations of these quantities to reduce
in amplitude.
This type of oscillation is shown in Figure \ref{fig:equilib}.
As a result, an equilibration period of around 250 ps is chosen before any measurements are taken.

\par
Anisotropic NPT simulations at a constant 3 GPa were done in the temperature range of the VII $\leftrightarrow$ VIII
phase boundary:
a `cooling run' for Ice--VII and a `heating run' for Ice--VIII, seeing if one (or both) would change
into the other.
The temperature ranged from 230-520K with increments of 10K. 
Although the actual phase boundary lies at around 270K at these conditions (see Figure \ref{fig:phase-diagram}),
as is typical for molecular dynamics simulations, significant hysteresis was expected, requiring temperatures 
to be significantly higher than the real-world value.
Furthermore, considering that the VII $\to$ VIII transition is a disorder $\to$ order transition, the
hysteresis was expected to be even more significant and the focus was placed on producing the easier VIII $\to$
VII transition.
Because MD is computationally intensive, and the equilibration period at each temperature was requiring
about 6 hours of computer time with 8 parallel cores, a temperature ramp \textit{i.e.} heating up the
same system slowly was impractical.
The best approach was to run simultaneous simulations at different temperatures and inspect the final
states of each.
However, a MD system which is started in the anistropic NPT system is generally not stable, since the molecules
have not had time to settle into their positions and for the pressure to become stable.
Hence, the simulation was first run in the NVT ensemble with the same Nosé-Hoover themostat for 10 ps until 
the molecular positions had adjusted accordingly from the generated structure.
Once this condition was satisfied, the pressure was gradually adjusted to the target 3 GPa.
This was done isotropically so that there were no unstable box deformations with pressure fluctuation.
Once the system reached the target pressure, it was allowed to undergo the equilibration period and
then run for a further 3.75 ns.
The full procedure for these runs is outlined graphically in the flowchart in Figure \ref{fig:flowchart}.
