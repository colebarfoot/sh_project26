\section{Introduction}

The character of planet Earth is defined by the abundance of H$_2$O (water) in all three of its phases: 
vapour, water, and ice.
Water currently covers 70\% of the globe, with 10\% of the land mass carpeted in its solid phase.
As a result, ice has long captivated scientists whose study of its properties has yielded fundamental 
insights in fields as diverse as glaciology and astrobiology.

\par
Water molecules are comprised of two hydrogen atoms (which may be labelled henceforth as protons) 
covalently bonded to a central oxygen atom in a non-linear arrangement where the free gaseous molecule 
has an H --- O --- H angle of 104.52$^\circ$ (Figure \ref{fig:water}).

\par
With its valence band structure having two half-filled electron orbitals, oxygen is commonly 
found in this type of tri-atomic structure.
Water is unique, however due to the intermolecular interactions labelled as `hydrogen bonds'.
This type of bonding occurs when a proton is present in between two of the highly electronegative
atoms (an atom that attracts electrons) Flourine, Nitrogen, or Oxygen.
Unlike permanent-dipole --- permanent-dipole attractive intermolecular forces which are present in similar
polar molecules, the hydrogen bond is particularly strong and accounts for the tetrahedral bonding
network in ice \cite{petrenko2002}.
Due to the flexibility of this hydrogen-bonding arrangement, ice can form a remarkable number of distinct
crystal phases, with more than twenty having been discovered since the early 20th century \cite{hansen2021}.

\par
In recent years Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations have become increasingly popular, allowing researchers to 
probe the properties of materials, such as ice, in extreme conditions without the need to set up expensive experiments.
In classical MD, atoms are treated as point particles with a mass in space whose interactions are modelled by 
`force fields': functions describing atomic potential energies and typically comprise several distinct terms 
such as bond energies, intramolecular bond bending and stretching, and intramolecular interactions such as dipole 
electrostatic repulsion/attraction and van der Waals (vdW) forces.
Since MD is particularly computationally intensive, early simulations used `fixed charge models' which considered
only pairwise Coulomb interactions between point-charge atoms with a partial charge $q_i$ fitted from experimental data
and a `mean-field polarization' determined from these $q_i$. 
Despite the known importance of `interactive' dipoles, \textit{i.e.} those affected by molecular configuration and 
the environment, these early models achieved surprising success \cite{riniker2018}. 
In the intervening years, however, computational resources have improved and more complex models have become
practical.

\par
Advances in modelling water have been made primarily by the introduction of `polarizable' force fields which possess
interactive dipoles.
The aim of this report is to model high-pressure ice with the Atomic Multipole 
Optimized Energetics for Biomolecular Applications (AMOEBA) force field.
Polarizable models which are able to model electrostatic interactions more accurately could have a profound effect
on the precision of simulated predictions.
Although AMOEBA has shown good agreement with experiment and first-principles calculation in less extreme conditions 
\cite{ren2003}, it is currently untested for high pressure.

\par
In order to assess its suitability for high pressure, AMOEBA ice will be simulated around the Ice--VIII/Ice--VII 
phase boundary in the 3 GPa region.
These phases of ice are particularly relevant as they occupy a significant portion of the phase diagram in
the region above 2 GPa (see Figure \ref{fig:phase-diagram}) and exhibit the densest packing of all
known arrangements.
Ice--VII and --VIII are not typically found on Earth, but it has been postulated that these crystals may be 
found in places as exotic as the distant moons of Europa and Callisto \cite{petrenko2002}.
Following this, AMOEBA Ice--VII will be heated at very high pressures (8 GPa) to investigate the structure
of a new high temperature phase of ice, plastic Ice--VII, which has recently been observed in diffraction 
experiments by Rescigno \textit{et al.} \cite{rescigno2025}.

\subsection{The Crystalline Structure of Ice}
Although ice has more than twenty known phases, the majority found on Earth is accounted for by a single phase: 
hexagonal Ice--I$_h$.
It is obtained by the freezing of liquid water at ambient conditions.
The properties and structure of Ice--I$_h$ were the first to be examined by researchers in this field.
Remarkably, this ice has lower density than its liquid counterpart, which is the cause of burst pipes in
winter and floating icebergs.
It is also characterised by a lack of long-range ordering in its protons, which gives rise to a residual
`zero-point entropy' in its configuration \cite{petrenko2002}.
These unique properties prompted the formalisation of the theory of how water molecules interact in solid 
phase.

\subsubsection{Ice Rules}
The `ice rules' which govern the crystalline arrangment of ice were first proposed 
by Bernal and Fowler in their \textit{Theory of Water and Ionic Solution} (1933) \cite{bernal1933}:
\textit{Every molecule is surrounded by four others in a tetrahedron and this arrangement will have least energy if the
H-nuclei, which subtend and angle of about $109^\circ$ at the centre of the molecule, lie opposite two of the
neighbours, while one of the H-nuclei of each of the remaining neighbours lies opposite a negative corner of the 
original molecule.}
As far as current research is aware, all known crystalline phases of ice obey these rules.
Note that these rules allow structures which are either proton-ordered or -disordered (as in Ice--I$_h$), so long 
as each water molecule's arrangement is in agreement with that of its neighbour.
These rules achieved their first success with Pauling's (1933) calculation of the zero-point entropy of Ice--I$_h$
whose structure can be described by hexagonal rings with an oxygen atom on each vertex in Figure \ref{fig:ice-ring}.

For Pauling's calculation, consider a mole of Ice--I$_h$ with $N$ molecules organised in these hexagonal rings. 
In the Bernal-Fowler scheme, an arbitrary water molecule can orient itself in 6 different ways. 
However, there are only two unoccupied tetrahedral directions for each oxygen. 
The chance that both directions can be aligned with the current molecular orientation is $\frac{1}{4}$ 
giving a total number of possible configurations and a configurational entropy given by Boltzmann's formula

\begin{equation}\label{eq:ice-ring-entropy}
    W = \left(\frac{6}{4}\right)^N = \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^N \quad\implies\quad S = Nk_B\log\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)
\end{equation}

where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant which gives a value of $S$ around 3.4 J mol$^{-1}$ K$^{-1}$.
This simple calculation proved to have remarkable agreement with experiment.
The hydrogen sites in disordered structures like these are said to be half-hydrogen sites, since the water molecules
are able to orient themselves with their hydrogen atoms in either of the microstates illustrated in 
Figure \ref{fig:ice-ring} with equal probability.
High-pressure states are believed to obey these ice rules \cite{ackland2025}.

\subsubsection{Ice--VII and Ice--VIII}
With reference to the phase diagram in Figure \ref{fig:phase-diagram} the two crystal phases that 
this report will focus on, Ice--VII and --VIII, form from around 2 GPa.
They are differentiated primarily by their long-range proton ordering: Ice--VII is disordered and Ice--VIII
is ordered \cite{nelmes1998,kuhs1984}.

\par
Ice--VII has a BCC (base-centred cubic) crystal structure which can be generated by
two interpenetrating, unconnected Ice--I$_c$ (the cubic version of Ice--I$-h$) lattices shown in Figure
\ref{subfig:ice7cell}.
The proton-disordering gives half-hydrogen sites which are denoted with half-filled circles.
Hence, Ice--VII has the same Pauling entropy as Ice--Ih given in \ref{eq:ice-ring-entropy}.

\par
Upon the proton-ordering transition to Ice--VIII, there is a distortion of the BCC unit cell as the
two interpenetrating lattices are shifted by $\epsilon$/2 into the orthorhombic structure in 
Figure \ref{subfig:ice8cell}, with a $\frac{c}{a}$ ratio of ~1.45.
This ratio is typically expressed as $\frac{c}{\sqrt{2}a}$ to encapsulate the deviation from
the BCC lattice.
Because of the complete proton-ordering, the hydrogen sites become full and the crystal is 
antiferroelectric (no net polarization) \cite{petrenko2002}.

\par
Plastic crystal phases are an intermediate between solids and liquids.
The properties of Ice--VII may allow its hydrogen atoms to rotate almost freely as if in a liquid at 
high temperatures.
The strongly directional hydrogen bonds which are characterised by Bernal and Fowler may suppress
such a state \cite{rescigno2025}.

\par
Knowledge of crystal structure is essential for interpreting results from either simulation or experiment.
With an understanding of ice molecular arrangements, predictions can be made about its properties.

\subsection{Molecular Dynamics}
Almost since the advent of computers, physicists have been attempting to implement some form of Molecular
Dynamics simulation.
Computational resources are improving and parameter fitting is
increasing in accuracy and as a result, the atomic potential functions (`force fields') are better at reproducing
physical results.

\par
Classical MD, the simplest and most prolific paradigm, treats atoms as point particles, setting up Newton's laws 
for the system with forces described by the force field, and integrating to obtain positions and velocities 
\cite{vollmers2025}.
As discussed earlier, these force fields contain many different terms describing how the atoms interact.
There are some interactions which are weaker than others, and in particular may be very time-consuming to compute,
and hence may be neglected.
Although some of these neglected effects may have theoretical significance, models such as fixed-charge models
have still found agreement with experiment \cite{guillot2002}.
However, atoms are known to exist in the `quantum world', and so implementing a classical approach
has certain drawbacks.
As computers have become more powerful, \textit{ab initio} MD simulations, which solve for quantum effects explicitly 
each timestep using electronic structure theories `on the fly' rather than using fixed potentials have become practical
\cite{iftimie2005}.
Since the timescale of certain interesting physical phenomena like phase transitions may be in the micro- to millisecond
order of magnitude (far greater than the typical MD timestep of 1 femtosecond) the strain placed on computational 
resources by complex \textit{ab initio} calculations may still prove to be too much.
Hence, classical MD simulations are the \textit{status quo} for large systems but with increasingly accurate 
force fields that include more complex effects and parameters determined by extensive first-principles calculation. 
This includes the `polarizable' models, like AMOEBA, which are the focus of this report \cite{halgren2001,finney2001}.

\par
In the evolution of the computer simulation of water, countless potentials have been developed,
the most notable of which are the TIP4P/TIP5P family.
Water models are typically calibrated against a few different metrics: liquid density, heat of vapourisation,
diffusivity, structure, density maximum, critical parameters, and dielectric constant \cite{guillot2002}.
These are the benchmarks for the success of a new model.

\subsection{AMOEBA Force Field}
The AMOEBA force field was designed to model the electrodynamic interactions between molecules
more accurately by including the effects of atomic charge distributions on neighbouring atoms.
This requires complex treatment of the electrostatics, which is necessarily computationally intensive.
The complete form of the AMOEBA potential is

\begin{equation}
    U = U_{\mathrm{bond}} + U_{\mathrm{angle}} + U_{\mathrm{b}\theta} + U_{\mathrm{oop}} + U_{\mathrm{torsion}}
    + U_{\mathrm{vdW}} + U^{\mathrm{perm}}_{\mathrm{ele}} + U^{\mathrm{ind}}_{\mathrm{ele}}
    \label{eq:amoeba}
\end{equation}

where short-range valence interactions: stretching, angle bending,
bond-angle cross term, out of plane bending, and torsional rotation are given by the first 5 terms
and the last three terms are the non-bonded vdW (van der Waals) and electrostatic contributions,
the description of which are given in Ren and Ponder's papers describing the original and updated versions of the
forcefield \cite{ren2003,ponder2010}.

\par
The first four bonding terms are from an earlier forcefield (MM3), designed for the treatment of hydrocarbons
\cite{allinger1989}.
They are anharmonic, using deviations from ideal bond lengths and angles up to as far as 6th order,
meaning that they allow for compression and expansion.
The torsional term is in the form of a Fourier expansion which smooths the potential in between the short-range and 
non-bonded interactions. 
This term has greater impact in the study of certain organic compounds, like proteins, which have more complicated
valence interactions, although it should still be effective in balancing AMOEBA water physically.

\par
The non-bonded interaction terms are more characteristic of AMOEBA and its classification as a `polarizable model'.
The vdW interaction is in a `buffered 14-7' form with a `site' for each atom, which is the point from which pairwise
interactions are calculated.
This is slightly less repulsive than the well-known Lennard-Jones (12-6) potential.
It was determined to have a better fit to state of the art calculations of noble gases.
The vdW site for the hydrogen atom, however is not actually on the atom itself, it is instead placed at 
a suitable distance along the O---H bond.
The use of such a `reduction factor' is fairly common among water potentials and has significant evidence from
calculation and experiment \cite{stewart1965}. 
%fix this!
However, one of the most common water potentials, TIP4P, has zero hydrogen vdW contribution \cite{abascal2005}.
The AMOEBA model accounts for the permanent multipole contributions of charge sites placed on the
atoms up to the quadrupole moment

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:polytensor}
    M_i = [q_i, u_{i,1}, u_{i,2}, u_{i,3}, Q_{i,11}, Q_{i,12}, ..., Q_{i,33}]^T.
\end{equation}

where $q$, $u_j$, and $Q_{jk}$ represent the point charge, dipole, and quadrupole moments, respectively.
This gives the interaction term 

\begin{equation}\label{eqn:perm-ele}
    U^\mathrm{perm}_\mathrm{ele}(r_{ij}) = M_i^TT(r_{ij})M_j
\end{equation}

where $T(r_{ij})$ is the some matrix differential operator and $r_{ij}$ is the separation of pairs of charge sites.
The local frame in which these moments are evaluated should be chosen carefully, respecting molecular symmetry
such that the multipoles remain constant with respect to their local frames as the molecules rotate and
bend and stretch.
For example, the $z$-axis with respect to the charge site on the oxygen atom of AMOEBA water is the 
H---O---H bisector, which remains invariant through all the transformations listed above.

\par
Before the publication of the AMOEBA force field, progress had been made towards a `polarizable' model for 
MD.
The earliest theoretical implementations of induced dipoles, the point dipole model,
suffered from a `polarizability catastrophe':
as the separation between point dipoles became increasingly small, atomic polarizablilities
diverged.
This is not observed physically.
Hence, Thole (1981) \cite{thole1981} proposed a modification to the point dipole model which damped short range dipole interactions
by treating one of the dipoles in each pairwise interation as a smeared charge distribution.
Implementing a Thole-style model is necessary for these MD potentials to allow intramolecular dipole induction
without unphysical diverences.
The `smearing' function for the AMOEBA model takes the form $\rho = \frac{3a}{4\pi}\exp(-au^3)$ where $a$ is
a `damping' parameter that describes the width of the smearing with value $a = 0.39$ for all molecules and 
$u = r_{ij}(\alpha_i\alpha_j)$ is a function of the separation and polarizability of pairs of atoms.
This smearing function is related to the interaction matrix of induced multipoles, with the first order element
matrix given by $T_i = -[1-\exp(-au^3)]R_i/R^3$.
Higher order elements are less trivial, but may be used to iteratively solve for induced dipoles 

\begin{equation}
    \mu^{\mathrm{ind}}_{i,\alpha}(n+1) = \mu^{\mathrm{ind}}_{j'}(n) + \alpha_iT^{ij'}_{\alpha\beta}\mu^{\mathrm{ind}}_{j'\beta}(n)
    \label{eqn:ind-dip}
\end{equation}

for $n = 0, 1, 2, ...$ and $\alpha, \beta = 1, 2, 3$ 
where the Einstein summation convention is used, $\mu^{\mathrm{ind}}$ are the induced dipoles,
$\alpha_i$ is the polarizability of an atom $i$ and $T$ is the interaction matrix.
The base case, $\mu^{\mathrm{ind}}_{\alpha}(0)$ is the induced dipole due to the permanent multipoles
of neighbouring charge sites.
Higher-order terms are `mutual induction', dipoles induced by neighbouring induced dipoles.
These mutual induction terms do converge to a sensible value, but it is the inclusion of this sort of
computation that reduces the speed of AMOEBA with respect to other, non-polarizable potentials.
However, for a periodic system, like ice crystals, electrostatic interactions in the AMOEBA model are treated
by the Ewald summation method which greatly increases calculation efficiency, the details of which are 
outlined in \cite{smith1998}.

\par
The AMOEBA model was initially developed to simulate liquid water.
Its parameters were fitted to data from the liquid and gas-phases, although previous experiments have shown AMOEBA 
water to be reasonably accurate at reproducing the properties of low-pressure ice \cite{ren2004}.
Earlier work has already been done on the mixing of liquid water and methane at high pressures \cite{kerr2024},
but these densely packed ice structures will test the limits of this potential.
