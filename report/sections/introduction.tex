Ice has long been an object of interest for scientists.
Its unique properties, particularly its lower density than its liquid phase counterpart, are perplexing.
Since the study of ice began in earnest in the early 20th century, more than twenty distinct 
crystal phases have been discovered.
Many methods have been employed in unravelling ice's mysteries, but in recent years Molecular Dynamics (MD)
simulations have become increasingly popular.
MD simulations allow researchers to probe the properties of substances in a range of conditions without 
setting up expensive experiments.
This makes MD simulations uniquely suited to investigate extreme conditions such as high pressure and 
high temperature environments.
In classical MD (the most common form), intermolecular interactions are modelled by 'force fields' which
are functions describing molecular potential energies.
Since MD is a particularly computationally intensive endeavour, early implementations were 'fixed charge
models' whose molecules exhibited no distortion of their charge distribution due to neighbouring atoms.
In the intervening years, however, computational resources have improved and more complex models have been
implemented.
Advances in modelling water have been made primarily by the introduction of 'polarizable' force fields: 
molecules whose charges are affected by the behaviour of their neighbours.
The modern model used in these simulations is the AMOEBA (Atomic Multipole Optimized Energetics for Biomolecular 
Simulation) force field which has been shown to be superior to certain models (TIP3P, ..., ...) in water, steam,
and low pressure ice.
However, AMOEBA is untested for high pressure ice (\textgreater1GPa regime).
An accurate model for ice at these conditions would be able to provide valuable insight into its behaviour and
properties.
In order to assess its suitability, AMOEBA ice will be simulated around the Ice--VIII/Ice--VII phase boundary
in the 2--3GPa region.
Following this, AMOEBA Ice--VII will be heated at very high pressures ($\sim$ 8GPa) to investigate the possibility 
of a new high temperature phase of ice: Ice--VIIp.
The results will then be compared to some common MD water models and experiment.

\subsection{The Crystalline Structure of Ice}
\begin{itemize}
    \item phase diagram
    \item crystal structures
    \item Bernal-Fowler ice rules
    \item Pauling entropy from first principles
\end{itemize}
There are many different phases of ice, spanning a wide range of conditions.
The structure of these phases is well-known by diffraction studies.
This variation should be compared to other molecules.
Similar ones (as known by chemists) are \textit{e.g.} $\text{SH}_2$.
The most common phase on Earth is hexagonal Ice--$1_h$.
This is the type of ice which one would find in an ice-cube tray.
It has certain properties which I will talk about briefly.
The polymorphism of the ice crystal is due to the flexibility of its hydrogen-bond network.
There is certainly more to be said about this.
The ices of interest in this examination of AMOEBA are differentiated by their proton
arrangements: ordered (Ice--VIII) and disordered (Ice--VIII).
Their crystal structures are described here.
Ice--VII exhibits a bcc (base-centred cubic) lattice with oxygens at something and the hydrogens 
elsewhere.
Its proton disordering produces ferroelectric effects (or ferromagnetic).
Ice--VIII exhibits an orthorhombic lattice.
Its proton ordering ensures that there is no net electric dipole moment.
It is interesting how these phases are paired across a straight-line phase boundary but exhibit
such different properties.

Consider a mole of ice with $N$ molecules. 
In the Bernal-Fowler structure, a water molecule can orient itself in 6 different ways. 
However, there are only two unoccupied tetrahedral directions for each oxygen and the chance
that both can be aligned with the current orientation is $\frac{1}{4}$ hence the total number of 
configurations of a hexagonal water ring is 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ice-ring-entropy}
    W = \left(\frac{6}{4}\right)^N = \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^N \quad\implies\quad S = k\log\left[\frac{3}{2}\right]^N
\end{equation}
